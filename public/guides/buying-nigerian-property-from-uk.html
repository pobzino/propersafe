Buying property in Nigeria from the UK: the practical playbook

Propersafe Verification Desk · Updated 10 June 2026

You're in London or Manchester; the land is in Abuja. Between you and it sit a five-to-six-hour flight, a currency conversion, and a chain of people you have to trust with your money. Thousands of UK-based Nigerians make this purchase every year — some flawlessly, some catastrophically. The difference is rarely luck. Here's the playbook.

The distance problem, stated honestly

Distance removes every natural defence a local buyer has: you can't stand on the plot, walk into the registry, or read a seller's face. Everything arrives through intermediaries — and almost everyone offering to help has a stake in the deal closing. That's not cynicism; it's the structure of the situation, and the entire playbook below is built to compensate for it.

1. Verify before any money moves — including the deposit

The full six-check process — registry search, document verification, court search, physical survey, seller authority, legal review — applies with extra force from abroad. Two UK-specific rules:

  • Even the "small refundable deposit to hold the plot" waits for verification. Deposits to unverified sellers are the single most common first loss — and the refund clause is only as good as the person holding the money.
  • Whoever verifies must be engaged by you and answerable only to you — not the seller's lawyer, not the agent's surveyor, not the cousin brokering the deal. Results come to you directly: stamped reports, not WhatsApp summaries.

2. Move money like it might be evidence — because one day it might

  • Use regulated transfer channels in your own name. Traceability protects you twice: against fraud now, and as proof of how the property was funded later — for resale, repatriating proceeds, or any future dispute.
  • Never route purchase funds through someone's personal account "to ease things." The moment money enters an intermediary's account, legally and practically it is theirs to misdirect.
  • Pay against milestones, not in one lump. Stage payments to verification, execution, and perfection stages; the exchange rate also tends to be kinder to staged transfers than to one panicked lump sum.
  • Keep everything: transfer receipts, the contract, the deed, chat threads. A complete paper trail is cheap insurance on both sides of the transaction.

3. Signing without flying

You don't need to be in Abuja to execute a purchase. The clean routes:

  • Direct execution — documents prepared by your lawyer and properly arranged for your signature, with originals exchanged through a controlled process.
  • A narrow power of attorney — if signing logistics genuinely require one, keep it surgical: this plot, these acts, this time window, drafted by your own lawyer. The family PoA that quietly covers "all my affairs in Nigeria" is how purchases become cautionary tales.
The separation principle: never let one person hold all three of — the power to sign, control of the money, and control of verification. Split those roles and most catastrophic outcomes become structurally impossible.

4. Finish the purchase — perfection is part of the price

From the UK, the strong temptation after the deed is signed is to relax: money sent, documents photographed, job done. But an executed deed without consent and registration is an unfinished purchase — and unfinished purchases discovered five years later, from five thousand kilometres away, are expensive to repair. Budget the perfection costs on day one and have your lawyer drive the application while the transaction is still warm.

5. The UK-specific traps

  • The diaspora sales event. Estate launches in London hotels and Instagram campaigns priced "for the diaspora" — glossy, urgent, and engineered to close before verification. The estate may be real; the parent title is the question, and it can be checked from right here before any commitment.
  • The December purchase. Buying during the Christmas visit compresses verification into two excited weeks. Start the checks from the UK months earlier; use the visit to see verified land, not to find land.
  • The helpful relative as project manager. Love and competence are different things — and accountability is a third. Family can be eyes on the ground; they should not be the verification, the signature, and the wallet at once.
  • Tax and records. Buying triggers no UK tax, but rental income generally must be reported in the UK, and a future sale can raise capital gains questions. Keep complete records and take advice from a UK tax adviser — early, not retroactively.

The condensed checklist

  1. Budget in pounds, including verification, professional fees, and title perfection — not just the land price.
  2. Verify everything before any money moves, through professionals who answer only to you.
  3. Transfer through regulated channels, in your name, against milestones.
  4. Sign through clean execution or a narrow, time-limited PoA — never an open-ended one.
  5. Perfect the title immediately: consent, stamping, registration.
  6. File every document and receipt on the UK side, twice.
