C of O vs R of O: Abuja title documents, explained

Sellers wave these acronyms around as if they settle everything: "It has C of O" is meant to end the conversation. It shouldn't. Here's what each document actually is, what it proves, what it doesn't — and the supporting papers that complete (or quietly undermine) a title.

Propersafe Verification DeskUpdated 10 June 20265 min read

First, the legal frame in one paragraph

Under Nigeria's Land Use Act, land isn't owned outright the way English freehold works. Land in the Federal Capital Territory is vested in the Federal Government and administered through the FCT authorities; what a "landowner" really holds is a right of occupancy — a long-term grant (commonly 99 years) with conditions attached. Every document below is evidence of that grant at a different stage of maturity.

R of O — Right of Occupancy

In Abuja practice, an R of O is the grant in its earlier form: the authority's offer of terms — the plot, the purpose, the term, the fees and development conditions. It's a genuine interest in land, but typically an unperfected one. Conditions may be unmet, fees may be outstanding, and the file hasn't ripened into a certificate.

  • What it proves: the named person was granted rights over a defined plot, on conditions.
  • What it doesn't: that conditions were met, that fees were paid, that the grant hasn't been revoked or reallocated — or that the paper in front of you is genuine.

C of O — Certificate of Occupancy

The C of O is the matured, registered certificate of that right — the strongest single title document an FCT plot ordinarily has. It carries the file and plot numbers, the cadastral zone, the term, and the covenants the holder must observe.

The trap: "it has C of O" tells you a certificate exists — not that it's genuine, not that the seller is the registered holder, not that it's free of mortgages, and not that the plot on the ground is the plot on the paper. Cloned C of Os are a staple of Abuja land fraud precisely because buyers treat the document as the finish line. It's the starting point: the certificate is only as good as the registry search that confirms it.

The supporting cast — where deals quietly fail

  • TDP / survey plan — defines the plot's actual coordinates and beacons. Without a surveyor confirming them on the ground, you can buy genuine papers for the wrong land.
  • Deed of assignment — the document transferring the interest to you. It needs proper execution and, critically, the granting authority's consent to perfect the transfer; skip that and you hold an incomplete interest.
  • Allocation letter — common in estate and government-scheme purchases; evidence of allocation, several steps short of a registered title. Ask what sits behind it.
  • Power of attorney — legitimate tool, favourite fraud vehicle. Anyone selling "on behalf of" an absent owner deserves the most scrutiny in this list.
  • Area council / customary allocations — Abuja's grey zone. Papers issued by area councils have historically had a contested status against FCT records, and many "cheap land" offers live exactly here. Some are regularisable; some are worthless. This category, above all, is not a do-it-yourself assessment.

What this means when you're buying

Don't ask "does it have C of O?" Ask: does the registry confirm this document, this plot, and this seller — today? That question is answered by an AGIS search, a beacon check, and a court search — the core of any real verification process — not by the quality of the paper or the confidence of the person holding it.
