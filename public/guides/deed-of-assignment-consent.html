Deed of assignment and consent, explained

Propersafe Verification Desk · Updated 10 June 2026

Most buyers treat the signing of the deed as the finish line: money paid, deed in hand, deal done. In the FCT, a signed deed is the middle of a purchase. What separates buyers who own their land from buyers who own paper is what happens after the signatures — consent and registration. This guide explains the whole sequence and the trap hidden in it.

What a deed of assignment is

The deed of assignment is the instrument by which the holder of a plot transfers their interest to you. A proper one identifies the parties, the plot (file number, plot number, district — matching the title documents), the consideration paid, and the root of the seller's own title, and it is executed by people who actually have authority to sign.

One Abuja nuance matters before anything else: in estates, what you're often offered is a deed of sublease rather than an assignment — the developer keeps the parent title and grants you a carved-out interest under it. Neither is inherently wrong, but they are different things, and which one you're signing determines what you'll actually own. Read the instrument, not just its title page.

The consent trap

The single most misunderstood step in Nigerian land transactions: because FCT land is held under rights of occupancy granted by the state, transferring that interest requires the consent of the granting authority to take effect. A deed without consent transfers far less than buyers believe — an inchoate interest that can be outranked by registered claims and is materially harder to defend or resell.

This is why "we'll handle consent later" is such an expensive sentence. Later has a way of becoming never: the seller loses interest once paid, costs are nobody's responsibility, and the buyer — often back abroad — holds an executed deed that was never perfected. Years on, at resale or in a dispute, the gap surfaces at the worst possible moment.

The full sequence, in order

  1. Verification first. The six checks — registry, courts, survey, seller authority — happen before any deed is signed, because a perfect deed from the wrong seller transfers nothing.
  2. Contract of sale. The commercial agreement: price, timeline, who bears which perfection costs. Decide the consent question here, in writing.
  3. Execution of the deed. Properly drafted, properly signed, properly witnessed — with original title documents handed over as agreed.
  4. Consent application. Through the FCT machinery, with its documentation and fees. Timelines vary; what matters is that the application actually goes in, with responsibility and funding agreed in advance.
  5. Stamping and registration. The transfer enters the public record — the step that puts the world on notice and protects you against later dealings on the same plot.

Where deals actually fail

  • The unregistered deed, resold. A seller with an unregistered transfer outstanding sells again; the second buyer who perfects first usually wins. Registration is not bureaucracy — it's the scoreboard.
  • The wrong signatory. Deeds executed by relatives, agents, or attorneys without authority — which is why seller-authority verification precedes signing.
  • The perpetual "processing". Consent costs left unallocated in the contract become consent never obtained.
  • The photocopy exchange. Paying in full while the seller "keeps the originals for now" — originals move when money moves, through a controlled exchange.

What to insist on as a buyer

  • A deed drafted or reviewed by a lawyer acting for you, not a recycled template from the seller's side.
  • The consent and registration plan — who applies, who pays, by when — written into the contract of sale.
  • A budget line for perfection costs from day one; they are part of the price of the land, not an optional extra.
  • Verification completed and current (searches refreshed within ~30 days) before execution and payment.
