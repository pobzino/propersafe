What is an AGIS search — and how does it work when you're abroad?

If you only run one check before buying Abuja land, this is the one. An AGIS search tests the seller's story against the government's own records — and it is the single check that defeats the largest share of land fraud.

Propersafe Verification DeskUpdated 10 June 20265 min read

What AGIS is

AGIS — Abuja Geographic Information Systems — is the Federal Capital Territory's computerised land registry, created in the early 2000s to digitise Abuja's land records into one cadastral database. Every properly allocated FCT plot has a file there: who holds it, its boundaries and particulars, and what interests are registered against it. When people talk about "doing a search" on Abuja land, this registry is what they mean.

What a legal search reveals

  • The registered holder — the name the government actually has on the plot, which either matches your seller's story or ends the conversation.
  • The plot's particulars — file number, plot number, district/cadastral zone — to be checked against the C of O or R of O in the seller's hand.
  • Registered encumbrances — mortgages, liens, and government interests that travel with the land regardless of what the seller discloses.
  • The status of the title — whether the record supports the document being shown to you at all.
Why it works: almost every title scam — cloned certificates, impersonated owners, revoked allocations — depends on you judging the deal by the seller's paperwork. The search replaces that paperwork with the registry's version of events.

How a search actually happens

  1. Gather the particulars. The plot and file numbers from the seller's documents are what the search runs on — a seller who won't share them is telling you something.
  2. Apply through the proper channel. In practice searches are conducted in Abuja, normally by a lawyer, with an application and the official search fee.
  3. Receive the result. The output is a search report — and where appropriate a certified true copy (CTC) of the record — stating what the register holds. Insist on the stamped result itself, not a WhatsApp summary of it.

Allow days rather than hours — and know the two-tier reality on the ground. Registries often offer a quick informal "window check"; the formal search letter takes longer and costs more, and it is the only result worth relying on for a purchase decision. Turnaround varies, so build that time into the deal rather than letting a seller's urgency compress it.

What a search can't tell you

The register is necessary, not sufficient. A clean search does not confirm that the land you were shown is the plot in the file (that's the surveyor's beacon check), that no court dispute is pending (that's a court search), or that the human selling is the person on the record (that's seller-authority verification). And where the "title" is an area council or customary allocation, the relationship with FCT records can be tangled — a category needing specialist judgment, not just a search. The full picture is the six-check process, of which this is the cornerstone.

From abroad, one trap matters more than all others: letting the seller's side "handle the search for you." A search you didn't commission, from a professional you don't control, is theatre. The result must come to you, from someone acting only for you.

Validity

A search is a snapshot. Titles get mortgaged, disputed, and transferred after searches are done — sometimes within the life of a single slow deal. Treat a result older than about 30 days as stale at the moment of payment, and refresh it if the transaction drags.
